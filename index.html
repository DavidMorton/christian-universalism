On Christian Universalism

A Personal Theological Outline
that God may be all in all
1 Corinthians 15:28
The view in summary

What It Is

Christian Universalism accepts most tenets of Christian tradition — the Trinity, the deity of Christ, that nobody comes to the Father but by Him — but rejects both eternal conscious torment and annihilationism. Instead, the afterlife is a place where all will be refined and prepared to take part in God's kingdom, with an eventual reconciliation of all to God, that Christ might be all in all.

Colossians 1:19-20 · Philippians 2:10-11 · 1 Timothy 4:10

Justification vs. Sanctification

There is a clear distinction between justification and sanctification. Each human is already justified on account of the blood of Christ, because Christ's sacrifice pays the penalty for all. Each person, however, has a choice about the timing of sanctification — we can be sanctified now or later, but sanctification will certainly happen.

Sanctification in eternity is God's judgment, and is always more severe than reconciliation on earth. Those who choose earthly sanctification will not experience the same level of judgment when faced with the glory of God, though some judgment remains. All will eventually be saved — though some as through fire.

1 John 2:2 · Romans 5:18 · 1 Cor 15:22 · Titus 2:11 · Romans 8:30 · Matt 5:25-26 · Mark 9:49 · Mal 3:2-3 · 1 Cor 3:14-15

So what's the point of evangelism?

If everyone is justified, why evangelize? Evangelism is still about bringing people to repentance — but not so they may be justified. The motive is to preach the good news of reconciliation so people might hear, repent, and begin living a life worthy of the gospel. This leads to experiential joy in this life (John 10:10) and to a reduced expectation of judgment (Hebrews 10:26–27). All people, believers and unbelievers, will still be judged by what they do.

Romans 14:10-12 · Acts 17:30-31 · 2 Cor 5:10 · Matt 25:31-32 · Rev 20:12

Where translation shapes theology

Notes on Terminology

On the word "Death"

The framework of ECT depends on a very specific understanding of death. In the traditional view, death is often equated with "eternal separation from God" — but this is pure eisegesis. Christian Universalism insists upon the plain meaning of the word, both in the straightforward physical sense and in the metaphorical sense of something being rendered powerless.

This is likely why many post-ECT Christian Universalists pass through annihilationism first. Choosing the plain meaning of a word immediately puts them out of step with the traditional view, which redefines the word to fit the target theology.

The Pervasive Influence of Tradition

One major stumbling block is the sheer number of interpretational reference works — Bible dictionaries, study Bibles, concordances, commentaries — that have absorbed the ECT viewpoint. The word "death," for example, may be explicitly defined in these works as "eternal separation from God."

A case study: Trench's Synonyms

Richard Trench's Synonyms of the New Testament compares two words for punishment: τιμωρία and κόλασις — the latter used in Matthew 25:46. In Classical Greek, κόλασις refers almost exclusively to corrective punishment. But to read it as corrective in Matthew would allow Universalism, so Trench declared it a "very serious error" to do so.

Contemporaneously, Trench's friend F.D. Maurice — an Anglican minister — had written that αἰώνιος likely refers to "of the age," and is qualitative rather than quantitative. Though Maurice denied being a Universalist, his view so threatened the establishment that he was dismissed from King's College for teaching opinions "calculated to unsettle the minds of the theological students."

One year later, Trench published his lexicon explicitly excluding correction as a meaning of κόλασις — rendering the αἰώνιος debate moot and forcing the retributive reading of Matthew 25:46.

Theological viewpoints have stickiness. We bring assumptions to the text (eisegesis), confirm them with resources (confirmation bias), and write new works carrying the same view — which become evidence for future works. Kuhn called this paradigm entrenchment; Gadamer called it the hermeneutical circle. Today we'd call it an echo chamber.

What This Means Hermeneutically

We are all subject to the echo chamber. A good student of the Bible ought to test traditional interpretations. The challenge is that the line between inspiration and interpretation is genuinely blurred. Something is always lost in translation. Scholars rise to clarify the ambiguous, and these clarifications become durable because of the echo chamber.

With God there is no "strength in numbers" if we are all wrong about ECT (Romans 3:4). If ECT is incorrect, then a large portion of the Western Christian world has cast a dark shadow on the love and mercy of God. For this reason — and because of Jesus' stark warnings against those who cause little ones to stumble — the burden of proof should lie with those who hold ECT.

What pushed the door open

Emotional & Philosophical Impetus

It would be dishonest to deny the emotional and philosophical challenges of holding to a viewpoint as inconsistent with God's character as ECT. These personal challenges spurred a deeper analysis of the text. In 2025 and 2026, I experienced what I can only describe as a deconstruction of the faith I held at the time (then, annihilationism). I could no longer have faith in a God who would destroy a large portion of the creation he had made.

In May of 2026 I encountered a book called The Christian Universalist. Almost overnight I was overjoyed — not only did God desire all to come to reconciliation, but he has made a way for it to happen. A God who is truly all-in-all ignited my then-laconic spirituality, and I wanted to see if this view could be honestly defended from the text. I was overjoyed to find that it can.

Objections that wouldn't resolve

Inconsistent with God's mercy
Eternal hell and annihilation don't fit God's revealed love and mercy.
Casts doubt on God's character
If God is merciful and yet cannot save, his omnipotence is in question.
Disproportionate punishment
Infinite punishment for finite sin violates eye-for-an-eye.
Reduces heavenly joy
When Thanos did it, it wasn't good. Heaven without grandma isn't as good.
Makes evil eternal
Eternal hell paired with strong free will makes evil a permanent feature of reality.
Ignores restorative justice
Given the choice between reconciliation and retribution — which does God prefer?

The Questions That Haunted Me

What of aborted babies?
ECT and annihilationism have no good answer — only "age of accountability" arguments with scant scriptural support. Christian Universalism has a ready and comforting answer.
What of those who never heard?
Partial reconciliation requires philosophical gymnastics here, from "they just go to hell, who are you to question" to throwing hands up entirely.
Does eternity hinge on an accident of timing?
Two men in a war. One dies by a bullet. The other lives to attend a Billy Graham revival. One goes to hell forever; one is saved. Really?
What of those unable to believe?
Some lack the mental capacity to hold faith. What of them?
What of my wayward child?
Under partial reconciliation, a mother never sees her wayward child again. He is damned forever.
Why is lazy Uncle Bob punished like Hitler?
ECT and annihilationism have to argue Uncle Bob receives the same fate as Hitler.

ECT offers no good answer to any of these questions. Indeed, these questions arise because of our collective adherence to the doctrine.

On the Charge of "Emotional" Reasoning

A common objection to Christian Universalism is that its proponents are led by emotion rather than careful exegesis. The charge proves too much.

The Reformed tradition has never treated emotion as inadmissible in theological reasoning. Edwards argued in Religious Affections that "true religion lies very much in the affections," and his preaching on hell — vivid imagery, saints rejoicing over the damned — is unmistakably emotional appeal in service of doctrine. Piper instructs preachers to "stir up holy affections" so hearers "feel the reality" of hell. Platt's missions appeals are built on visceral horror. The tradition cannot leverage emotion when it serves ECT and disqualify it when it raises questions about ECT. That is special pleading.

The framing of "reason versus emotion" is also un-Reformed. If total depravity is true, sin distorts the mind as thoroughly as the heart. Calvin himself spoke of the noetic effects of the Fall. The appeal to "reason over emotion" in hermeneutics borrows from Enlightenment rationalism, not Scripture. Both faculties are fallen; both require sanctification; both are legitimate inputs when submitted to the text.

Scripture itself never treats emotions as monolithic. It distinguishes them by source and orientation: sinful emotions arise from self-oriented desire (James 4:1), while compassion, grief, and longing for justice are commanded postures throughout the canon.

Inspired models

When Paul writes of his "great sorrow and unceasing anguish" over his kinsmen's possible damnation (Romans 9:2–3), and Christ weeps over Jerusalem (Matthew 23:37), these are inspired models of how a holy heart responds to the prospect of human destruction.

This reframes the universalist's engagement entirely. It is not a self-oriented attempt to soften God, but:

Grief that image-bearers would suffer forever
A sense that finite sin receiving infinite punishment violates proportionality
Longing for God's glory to be vindicated through reconciliation rather than division

These map directly onto the emotional postures Scripture commends in Paul and Christ Himself. Furthermore, texts about God's character — that God is love (1 John 4:8), desires all to be saved (1 Timothy 2:4), and is not willing that any should perish (2 Peter 3:9) — are themselves the text. Weighing them against judgment passages is exegesis, not sentiment.

The proper test is not whether emotion is present — it always is, on both sides — but whether it drives one toward Scripture or away from it. A sinful reaction avoids the text; a sanctified reaction drives deeper, wrestling with difficult passages and weighing the witness of the whole canon. The emotion confirms what the exegesis suggests; it does not replace it.

ECT Cannot Coexist with Assurance of Salvation

Evangelicalism (especially Reformed branches) grants assurance of salvation through "once saved always saved" — but this assurance rests on a foundation the system itself undermines.

The same tradition affirms:

Vessels of wrath
God creates vessels of wrath prepared for destruction (Romans 9:22).
Temporary faith
The reprobate may experience a "temporary faith" indistinguishable from the real thing — a problem Edwards and the Puritans worried over at length.
Hidden vs. revealed will
God's secret will may diverge from his revealed will.
Waiting for assurance
The Westminster Confession concedes that true believers may wait long years for assurance — and may lose it altogether.

For the self-assured, these tensions remain abstract. The doctrine functions because the believer never seriously entertains being on the wrong side of it. But for those whose experience has taught them that their own perceptions can deceive them, the tensions become unbearable, and rightly so. They are not malfunctioning; they are reading the doctrine correctly — and arguably more deeply than the self-assured believers. They are the canary in the mine.

The reasoning runs like this. Told to "just trust God" when their moral intuitions recoil at hell, they learn to treat their moral intuitions as unreliable. But once that habit is formed, it cannot be quarantined. It turns inward on their confession of faith itself.

Did I really believe? Was it enough? How would I know?

The doctrine of perseverance offers no exit, because the question is precisely whether one was ever truly in. Here ECT generates its own worst nightmare. To appeal to God's goodness as a guarantee against the worst-case scenario, one must first believe God is good — but ECT has already conceded that God is willing to inflict unending torment on conscious beings. The character question is closed before the assurance question is opened.

The unspeakable scenario

What principled restraint, then, prevents the deepest cruelty: a life lived in hopeful belief of election, revealed at judgment to have been the elaborate setup of a vessel of wrath? What torment could be greater than that?

The doctrine, in other words, eats its own assurance. It asks the believer to trust the character of a being whose character it has just impeached. For the self-assured, this contradiction is suppressed through psychological habit. For more sensitive souls, it cannot be. Fear is the rational response to a two-faced God.

Christian Universalism, then, is not wishful thinking but the recovery of coherence. It is the claim that God's character, as revealed in Christ, is what it appears to be — and that the gap between the revealed God and the hidden God, which ECT requires and which destroys assurance, does not in fact exist.

In sum

The question is never whether emotion belongs in theological reasoning — it always does, on every side of every debate — but whether a given emotion is rightly sourced and rightly oriented.

Emotions arising from self-protection, fear of consequence, or resistance to God's revealed character are properly suspect. But emotions arising from the Spirit's renewal of the heart, oriented outward toward God's glory, the flourishing of image-bearers, and the vindication of justice, are precisely the affections Scripture commands and Christ Himself models.

Grief over the prospect of eternal human destruction, longing for God to be truly "all in all," and a sense that infinite punishment for finite sin dishonors rather than glorifies God — these are not sentiments to be repented of. They are the cruciform affections of a heart being conformed to Christ, and they belong fully within the work of careful, faithful exegesis.

Social Consequences of ECT

"If your view of God is twisted, then the more devout you are, the worse off you are."

— Dallas Willard

We learn the character of God through his revealed word, and we are made in his image. People become like the God they believe. Beware how you see God — it can change who you are.

Andrea Yates

Many are unaware that Andrea Yates, who killed her five young children in a bathtub in 2001, did so because of her belief in eternal conscious torment. Her primary concern was that she would be unable to lead her children to saving faith. Because she believed in the "age of accountability," she reasoned that killing them before that age would save them from hell forever. Sadly, other than calling her psychotic, if the assumptions of ECT are true, her act was a perfectly reasonable one.

American Retributive Justice

American evangelicalism leans heavily toward ECT, and it begs the question: why does justice in the United States lean so heavily on retributive vs. restorative justice? A full examination is out of scope here, but it does tend to look like an artifact of our theological assumptions.

Responding to Pro-ECT Verses

Objection: "Universalist tendencies are selfish and sinful"

This depends on motive. Some argue for universalism because they want license to sin — but they misunderstand Christian Universalism, which requires reparations for sin even in the afterlife. They likely hold to a pluralistic universalism instead.

For many others, Christian Universalism is not about getting away with sin but about the hope of being with their loved ones forever. It isn't selfish to want your friends to experience good things.

The trilemma

These three claims cannot all be true:

  1. God is good and wants everyone reconciled to him.
  2. God has the power to achieve any goal he desires.
  3. Not everybody will be reconciled.

ECT proponents say we should accept the contradiction as a mystery. Perhaps. But if Scripture can be read in a way that avoids the dilemma — why wouldn't we?

Objection: "It denies free will"

If a child makes a poor decision, a good parent doesn't shrug and "honor his free will." A good parent saves the child and aims to correct him. We overindex on free will. God is everyone's father — even of those who deny it — and he will meet ignorance with knowledge and lead all to redemption eventually.

✦

Matthew 25:41, 46

"Depart from me, you who are cursed, into the eternal fire prepared for the devil and his angels... Then they will go away to eternal punishment, but the righteous to eternal life."

Matthew 25:46 is arguably the strongest argument for ECT. The whole weight rests on two Greek words.

αἰώνιος — "eternal" or "of the age"?

For ECT adherents, αἰώνιος is synonymous with everlasting. But scripturally this isn't always the case. The actual meaning is "of the age," and context decides whether it lasts the entire age or is something of an age that eventually ends.

Romans 16:25-27

"The mystery hidden for long (αἰώνιος) ages past, but now revealed."

Clearly bounded — the αἰώνιος ages ended.
2 Timothy 1:9

"Grace given us in Christ Jesus before the beginning of time (πρὸ χρόνων αἰωνίων)."

A starting point — not endless from eternity past.
Jude 7

"Sodom and Gomorrah... serve as an example of those who suffer the punishment of eternal (αἰώνιος) fire."

The fire isn't still burning.
Genesis 49:26 (LXX)

"Your father's blessings are greater than the blessings of the ancient (αἰώνιος) mountains."

Used spatially/qualitatively, not durationally.
Numbers 25:13 (LXX)

"A covenant of a lasting (αἰώνιος) priesthood."

Habakkuk 3:6 (LXX)

"The ancient (αἰώνιος) mountains crumbled."

They crumbled — so αἰώνιος clearly isn't 'endless' here.

In Scripture, αἰώνιος does not always mean everlasting. It can mean "of the age." Linguistically, it's a toss-up.

κόλασις — retributive or restorative?

In Aristotle's Rhetoric 1.10.17, he explicitly distinguishes τιμωρία from κόλασις: the former is for the sake of the one inflicting it; the latter for the sake of the one suffering it. κόλασις is corrective.

A caveat: Aristotle wrote ~350 years before Jesus, and the word likely carried more connotations by the New Testament era. ECT proponents push this to say the corrective sense is no longer the meaning — but the classical definition remains a valid one, even if additional senses developed.

So the word can mean either restorative or retributive justice.

The four-cell matrix

κόλασις = Retributive κόλασις = Restorative
αἰώνιος = Everlasting ECT Nonsensical*
αἰώνιος = Of the Age Leans Annihilationism Christian Universalism
*Could root in restorative justice flowing eternally from God, but is awkward enough to be unlikely.

By itself, Matthew 25:46 is roughly 50/50 between ECT and Christian Universalism — with annihilationism uphill. Read against the larger witness of Scripture, restorative justice of the age is the most coherent reading: when God separates the sheep from the goats, some go on to eternal life, and some to a punishment in that age.

Eternal Fire — Matthew 18:8, Mark 9:43-48

"It is better for you to enter life maimed or crippled than to have two hands or two feet and be thrown into eternal fire... where the worms that eat them do not die, and the fire is not quenched."

Both verses are intentionally hyperbolic and so are unreliable witnesses to the durative nature of the fire. Moreover, it is the fire that is unquenchable, not the individual's existence in it. The rest is reader's inference.

✦

Rich Man and Lazarus — Luke 16:23-24

"In Hades, where he was in torment, he looked up and saw Abraham far away, with Lazarus by his side. So he called to him, 'Father Abraham, have pity on me and send Lazarus to dip the tip of his finger in water and cool my tongue, because I am in agony in this fire.'"

A bizarre passage for several reasons — it's the only parable with a named individual, and it has close associations with an Egyptian story circulating at the time. Using this to ground a doctrine of the afterlife is a literary misuse. The Egyptian source held a similar view of the afterlife, and the point of the passage is the Pharisees' resistance to believing in Christ — that they wouldn't believe even if someone returned from the dead. Not a strong support for ECT once the literary considerations are weighed.

✦

2 Thessalonians 1:8-9

"...in blazing fire... He will punish those who do not know God and do not obey the gospel of our Lord Jesus. They will be punished with everlasting destruction and shut out from the presence of the Lord and from the glory of his might."

This is one of those verses where translator bias bleeds into the text. Because traditional theology assumes a holy God cannot be in the presence of sinners, this is rendered as punishment away from God.

The Greek ἀπό problem

This is the only place in most English New Testaments where ἀπό is translated "away from." Everywhere else, it means "emanating from," unless a verb clarifies otherwise (e.g., hiding from). There is no such clarifying verb in the original Greek of 2 Thess 1:9.

The "emanating from" reading would render the verse: destruction emanating from the presence of the Lord — which is consistent with refining fire.

It also directly contradicts Revelation 14:9-11, where the worshippers of the beast are tormented in the presence of the holy angels and the Lamb.

✦

Revelation 14:9-11

"...they will be tormented with burning sulfur in the presence of the holy angels and of the Lamb. And the smoke of their torment will rise for ever and ever. There will be no rest day or night for those who worship the beast and its image..."

The smoke rising forever is a literary callback to the total destruction of cities in the Old Testament. Sodom's smoke is said to go up forever — but it isn't rising today, and it wasn't rising when Jude wrote his book. To take this literally is to abuse the genre.

✦

Revelation 20 — The Lake of Fire

"And the devil, who deceived them, was thrown into the lake of burning sulfur, where the beast and the false prophet had been thrown. They will be tormented day and night for ever and ever... Then death and Hades were thrown into the lake of fire. The lake of fire is the second death. Anyone whose name was not found written in the book of life was thrown into the lake of fire."

I won't spend much time on these — it's not humans being punished in Rev 20:10. It's the devil, the beast, and the false prophet. Even death and Hades are thrown in. The eternal torment language applies to these figures, not to humanity.
